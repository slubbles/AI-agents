The psychology of the person reading it
They posted a job listing. They're expecting applications. Their brain is in hiring mode — evaluating candidates, thinking about salary, wondering if someone can do the work long-term.
Your message arrives and it's not that. So the first job of your message is a pattern interrupt — something that immediately signals "this is different, read this differently." Not with a gimmick. With immediate relevance.
The person posting that job has one dominant thought: I need this thing built and I need to trust whoever builds it. Your message needs to address both in the first two sentences. What you're building and why they should trust it's going to get done right.
The structure that works
One opening line that proves you read their specific post. Not "I saw your job listing" — that's generic. Something like "You're looking for someone to build a Next.js marketing site with SEO — I've done this exact thing and I want to offer you something better than hiring."
That line does three things. It shows you read their post. It signals you have relevant experience. And it creates curiosity about what "better than hiring" means without being clickbaity.
Second — the reframe. Short. Two or three sentences maximum. "Instead of hiring a full-time developer or freelancer on hourly rates, I offer this as a fixed-scope service. You know exactly what you're getting, exactly what it costs, and exactly when it's done. No surprises."
This is important because you're not just offering an alternative — you're making the alternative feel safer and more predictable than what they were planning to do.
Third — the specific deliverable. This is where most pitches go vague and die. Don't say "a production-ready Next.js site." Say exactly what's included. Something like:
"Here's what you get: fully responsive Next.js site, up to 5 pages, on-page SEO optimization including meta tags and structured data, deployed to Vercel, connected to your domain, with a CMS so you can edit content yourself. Delivered in 5 business days."
When someone reads a list that specific they think — this person has done this before. They know what it involves. They're not going to come back to me asking what I meant by "landing page."
Fourth — the friction removal. The biggest fear when someone gets an unsolicited service offer is: what if I pay and they disappear or deliver garbage? Address it directly. "I don't ask for full payment upfront. You pay half to start, half when you approve the final delivery. If you're not satisfied I'll revise until you are or refund you."
That one sentence removes the biggest objection before they even form it.
Fifth — the single clear next step. Not "let me know what you think." Not "feel free to reach out." One specific action. "Reply to this message with your project details and I'll send you a detailed proposal within 24 hours." Or even simpler — "Reply with yes and I'll send you the full scope document today."
Make it so easy to say yes that the only reason not to is genuine disinterest.
The length
Short enough to read in 90 seconds. Long enough to answer the three questions every buyer has — what do I get, can I trust this, what does it cost. If you can't fit it in 200-250 words you're including something that doesn't need to be there.
